Introduction

Compassion is at the forefront of national and international policy, practice and educational debates (Lown and Manning 2010, Cummings and Bennett 2012, Local Government Association et al 2012, Moore et al 2012). This can be seen in documents that promote models of practice which centre on relationships, and the need to nurture and sustain fundamental person-centred skills and values (Goodrich and Cornwell 2008). The emphasis on compassion is set in an increasingly complex healthcare context, which is often dominated by concerns about outcomes, efficiency, productivity and competence (Gray and DaSilva 2010, Parliamentary Health Service Ombudsman 2011), alongside an increasing ageing population and a rise in long-term conditions. However, there is consensus that care and compassion remain integral to health and social care (Cummings and Bennett 2012, Reid 2012).

While increased value is placed on compassion and caring, there is little understanding about how it can be promoted in the healthcare setting. This module discusses the concept of compassionate care and focuses on key processes that can support healthcare professionals in delivering this kind of care. It draws on the Leadership in Compassionate Care Programme, a three-year research project aimed at embedding compassion in education and practice, undertaken at Edinburgh Napier University and NHS Lothian in Scotland (Edinburgh Napier University and NHS Lothian 2012).

Defining compassion

The literature on compassion emphasises the relational nature of the concept (Paulson 2004, Sabo 2006, Sanghavi 2006) – compassion revolves around the way in which people relate to each other. It is not so much about what people choose to do for each other, but what they choose to do together. Therefore compassion implies a level of reciprocity and interdependence. Making explicit which dimensions of care comprise compassion is challenging because of the often invisible nature of this work. Pearson (2006) referred to the invisibility of small acts of compassion as ‘simple not clever; basic not exquisite; peripheral not central’.

Such acts are thought to be important, but in reality may have little currency for those managing and developing services. These acts are often more noticeable by their absence. The invisibility surrounding the concept of compassion has significant implications for its recognition, promotion and assessment in practice.

Definition

Compassion has been defined as ‘a deep awareness of the suffering of another coupled with a wish to relieve it’ (Chochinov 2007) and ‘a deep feeling of connectedness with the experience of human suffering that requires personal knowing of the suffering of others, evokes a moral response to the recognised suffering and that results in caring that brings comfort to the sufferer’ (Peters 2006).

Not everyone would describe day-to-day acts of compassion as being preceded by noticing suffering. Suffering could be seen as a response to some sort of extreme crisis. Therefore, it may be more appropriate to view compassion as a response to vulnerability in others, as this more accurately reflects compassion in practice (Dewar 2011).

Key dimensions of compassionate care drawn from the literature are that (Von Dietze and Orb 2000, Kanov et al 2004, Congressi 2006, Cash 2007, Kim and Flaskerud 2007, Schantz 2007, Schulz et al 2007, Youngson 2008):

  • It is a subjective experience.
  • It is about the quality of the relationship between individuals.
  • It is about relating to the needs of others.
  • It requires that individuals acknowledge the person with the illness.
  • It is about human experience and preserving the integrity of the individual.
  • It recognises suffering and vulnerability.
  • It requires emotional connection and interpersonal skills.

There is much debate about the term compassion and how compassion can best be supported, valued and assessed in practice. Box 1 offers a definition of compassionate care. Some processes have been highlighted as enabling compassionate care, such as developing interpersonal skills and supporting people to connect emotionally with patients to form meaningful relationships (Fosbinder 1994).

Box 1. Defining compassionate care

Compassionate care is a relational activity that is about ‘the way in which we relate to other human beings when they are vulnerable. It can be nurtured and supported. It involves noticing another person’s vulnerability, experiencing an emotional reaction to this, and acting in some way with the person, in a way that is meaningful for people. It is defined by the people who give and receive it, and therefore interpersonal processes that capture what it means to people, are an important element of its promotion’.

(Dewar et al 2011)

These dimensions and processes provide some insight into what is needed in practice to deliver compassionate care. However, there is more scope to examine the concept of compassion as it relates to all those involved, including self, patients and their families, and colleagues.
