Being compassionate with colleagues

Literature tends to prioritise the needs of patients in relation to caring. However, there also needs to be a focus on healthcare professionals. As Smith (2008) identified ‘nurses need to feel safe and cared for so they in turn can care for patients’. There is an important link between staff wellbeing and the quality and safety of care delivered (Boorman 2009, Dawson 2009).

If, as some authors suggest, one needs to work in a compassionate care environment to be able to deliver compassionate care (McQueen 2000, Gallagher 2004, Finfgeld-Connett 2008), there is a need to explore how compassion can be achieved for staff as well as patients and families. It is also important to consider whether the interpersonal processes suggested by the empirical work focusing on patient perspectives are similar to those that would help to support staff to receive compassion from their colleagues.

Examples of behaviours and actions that illustrate caring for each other as a team include (Dewar 2011):

  • Going out of your way to remember the names of new members of staff rather than using terms such as ‘the student’ or ‘the bank staff’.
  • Giving positive, specific feedback to others such as ‘I feel really safe and calm when you are in charge of the ward’, ‘I value the little things that you do for patients such as bringing shower gel in for the man who had none and had no relatives to do this for him’.
  • Noticing when others are not feeling at their best and working together with them to support them.
  • Helping people to come to their own understanding about how to deal with a situation rather than just telling them what to do.
  • Considering other perspectives rather than just thinking what you believe in is right.
  • Feeling brave enough to challenge behaviour that focuses on negativity and bickering.

Much of what is illustrated in the behaviours and actions above focuses on the way in which people communicate with each other in the team. It is about noticing, feeling and responding compassionately to colleagues. Dewar and Nolan (2013) identified the key process that encapsulates these actions as engaging in appreciative caring conversations with staff, patients and families to support compassionate relationship-centred care.

The way in which practitioners communicate with each other, and patients and families is fundamental to compassionate care. This has implications for how professionals teach these relational skills, which include asking curious questions, listening, responding together, negotiating and celebrating.

Doane (2002) stated that relational practice is a skilled process of inquiry and recognised the need to move away from mechanistic models of teaching behavioural communication, such as listening and questioning aimed at goal-directed communication and problem solving. Instead, models should focus on a process of inquiry that demonstrates an appreciation of people’s connectedness and emphasises being with people rather than doing things for them. The framework of the 7Cs of caring conversations (Table 1) is useful for developing the interpersonal skills necessary to enhance compassion (Dewar 2011, Dewar and Nolan 2013).

The compassionate organisation

The process of compassion is more than an individual experience. Parker (2002) stressed the importance of the wider organisation in creating a caring and compassionate culture. Evidence suggests that when compassion is evident throughout organisations it helps people feel valued, listened to and less alone (Kanov et al 2004). Current literature highlights that culture is important in changing practice in relation to caring (Dewar 2007, McCormack et al 2008, Powell et al 2009, Smith et al 2010).

Organisational models

‘Perform or peril’ model: Patterson et al (2011) (Table 2) described current care cultures as adopting a ‘perform or peril’ model dominated by hierarchical ways of working, weak leadership, a punitive culture and an emphasis on task rather than process, which creates impoverished care environments that are less likely to achieve high quality care.

Relational and responsive model: in contrast, Patterson et al (2011) emphasised the need for a relational and responsive model that paid attention to people and their perceptions.

Focusing on people and perceptions is emphasised in Youngson’s (2008) action plan for compassion, which includes:

  • Declaring compassion as a core value.
  • Rewarding rather than punishing compassionate care.
  • Honing compassionate and relationship skills.
  • Creating a safe place for meaningful conversation in the workplace.
  • Challenging models of professionalism.
  • Defining compassion as a management and leadership skill.

This action plan is a good starting point that questions how the norms, activities, policies and systems of the organisation align with the values of compassion. Activities that can begin to develop these actions in the organisation are detailed in Table 3.

Learning points

  1. One needs to work in a compassionate care environment to be able to deliver compassionate care. There is a need to explore how compassion can be achieved for staff as well as patients and families.
  2. Relational practice is a skilled process of inquiry. It recognises the need to move away from mechanistic models of teaching behavioural communication, and emphasises being with people rather than doing things for them.
  3. The framework of the 7Cs of caring conversations is useful for developing the interpersonal skills necessary to enhance compassion. The 7Cs are: being courageous, curious, and collaborative, connecting emotionally, considering other perspectives, compromising and celebrating.
  4. When compassion is evident throughout organisations it helps people feel valued, listened to and less alone.
  5. The core values for developing compassionate organisations are: rewarding compassionate care, honing compassionate and relationship skills, creating a safe place for meaningful conversation in the workplace, challenging models of professionalism, and defining compassion as a management and leadership competence skill.
