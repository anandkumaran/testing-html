Being compassionate with self

In 1994, Tresolini and Pew-Fetzer Task Force recommended that clinicians embrace relationship-centred care, which involves communicating openly with patients, and practising with a healing and caring ethic. They argued that practitioners’ self-recognition is crucial to the delivery of effective care and that ‘…without such self-knowledge a practitioner’s own emotional response to patients may act as a barrier to effective care’ (Tresolini and Pew-Fetzer Task Force 1994).

Encouraging healthcare professionals to practice self-compassion

It is clear from the literature that delivering compassionate care is a complex process which involves understanding the experience of giving and receiving care.

  • Practitioners need space and support to reflect on self in the care-giving experience.
  • Healthcare professionals need to learn to notice their thoughts and feelings as they arise and develop the ability to tolerate, rather than avoid, difficult feelings, memories or situations.
  • An awareness of healthcare professionals’ own emotions, and acting in a way that feels compassionate to both the caregiver and the patient, requires a level of emotional attunement (Buchanan-Barker and Barker 2004, Finfgeld-Connett 2008).
  • Staff need to be supported emotionally if they are to feel safe to engage in meaningful conversations necessary for compassionate care (Youngson 2008, Dewar et al 2010).

Other literature highlights the compassion fatigue and emotional burden that can affect the caregiver (Kooker et al 2007). While this may be the case, it is important to acknowledge that giving compassionate care can be a significant source of pleasure for caregivers and can be beneficial to individuals’ mental wellbeing. This is supported by authors who argue that caring is not an emotional burden, but a privilege that practitioners enjoy and that contributes to job satisfaction (Graber and Mitcham 2004, Pearson 2006, Kim and Flaskerud 2007, Youngson 2008). Graber and Mitcham (2004) discussed how, in their study, clinicians felt that the expression of compassion sustained and supported them rather than tiring or weakening them.

Being compassionate with patients and their families

Developing relationships, emotional connections, interpersonal skills and knowing the person with the illness have been identified as key processes that enable compassion to be realised in practice. Key dimensions of the experience of good care as perceived by patients include (Chochinov et al 2002, Walsh and Kowanko 2002, Matiti and Trorey 2008):

  • Being able to express emotions such as suffering and vulnerability.
  • Being valued and having individual needs recognised.
  • Being given choice and opportunities to be involved in care and decision making.
  • Being respected.
  • Being cared for by kind, warm and genuine staff.
  • Being given time by healthcare professionals.
  • Being given information by staff.

Although dimensions of caring that are valued by patients have been identified, studies have yet to clarify the process involved in delivering compassionate care. Central to the dimensions described above is the process of knowing the person. However, Dewing (2004) suggested that while person-centred and relationship models for caring emphasise the importance of knowing the person, this is skilled work that demands expertise. It is less clear from the literature what these skills comprise.

Interpersonal processes

Daaleman et al (2008) produced a model in the United States for developing interpersonal processes that promote spirituality at the end of life (Figure 1). They described three interpersonal processes, including:

  • Being present – giving attention to the emotional, social and spiritual needs of the person.
  • Opening eyes – understanding the person, their unique experience of illness and their life story.
  • Co-creating – generating a plan of care by mutual activity, focusing on maintaining the patient’s humanity and dignity.
Figure 1

These interpersonal processes are similar to the key processes needed to achieve compassionate relationship-centred care identified by Dewar (2011), including:

  • Engaging in conversation to understand who patients are and what matters to them.
  • Engaging in conversation to know how patients feel about their experience of care.
  • Working together to shape the way things are done.

These processes are also relevant for staff and patients’ families. Dewar and Nolan (2013) suggested that people do all of the above through appreciative caring conversations. The case study in Box 2 illustrates these key processes in action.

Box 2. Case study example to illustrate compassionate, relationship-centred care

‘One of the patients on the ward had been in for a few days after having developed increased confusion. The woman was blind and was being cared for in a single room as it was suspected that she had an infection. She appeared distressed most of the time and called out for people. We were concerned about her distress. I went into her room and asked her how she was feeling – she called out and her face grimaced. I asked her if there was something that would help us to care for her and she said “John 14”. I went into her room and asked her how she was feeling – she called out and her face grimaced. I asked her if there was something that would help us to care for her and she said “John 14”. She wanted to be read an excerpt from the Bible. I felt nervous about doing this as I was not religious and wondered if I would do this in a way that was appropriate for her. I told the woman how I felt and suggested that I asked the chaplain to come to do this with her. She said no she wanted me to read the Bible to her. She had given me the courage to give it a go.

I realised there were no longer any Bibles in the lockers. They had been removed some time ago because of equality and diversity issues, and infection control. Other staff tried to locate a Bible on neighbouring wards and found no one had one. They finally went to the chaplaincy centre and managed to retrieve one from a large storage cupboard full of Bibles. Reading the Bible had a direct effect on the patient’s wellbeing, as was evident in the fact that she became calm and less agitated.

Following discussions with the chaplain, copies of the Bible and Koran were reinstated in all wards in the hospital. It would have been easy for staff to fall at the first hurdle with this example, but they persevered. Staff did worry that they would not always be able to provide the aspects that patients felt were important to them, making them feel that asking the question in the first instance involved taking a risk. It could feel safer not to ask.’

(Dewar 2011)

Asking questions aimed at finding out what matters to people and how they feel about their experience is not always easy and staff need support to do this. In the case study example in Box 2, the nurse asked how the patient felt and shared with the patient how she felt. Sharing emotion in the nurse-patient interaction can be viewed as unprofessional. Stickley and Freshwater (2002) referred to ‘rehabilitating’ the emotions deemed to be inappropriate back into the nurse-patient relationship, so that healthcare professionals change from adopting a model of keeping one’s distance to one of meaningful engagement. It is this meaningful engagement that supports the delivery of compassionate care.

Through expressing emotions, the patient and staff member in the case study example exposed mutual vulnerability. Findings from Dewar and Nolan’s (2013) study demonstrated that sharing how people – patients, relatives or staff – feel about their experience, helped others understand the person and themselves in the context of health care.

The questions in Box 3 can be used to elicit important information that can assist people to give compassionate care. These questions can be asked of patients and families.

Box 3. Questions to help to get to know the person

  • What matters to you most while you are in hospital?
  • Tell me something that will help us to care for you here?
  • How do you feel about your experience?
  • What helps you to feel upbeat (positive) and well?
  • How would you like us to respond if you are feeling low?
  • Who are the most important people to you?
  • What worries or concerns do you have?
  • What things have worked well for you here?

(Dewar 2011)

It is through engaging with people in this way that practitioners can find out what matters to them and understand how they feel. This, in turn, can help healthcare professionals to work with people in a way that minimises vulnerability. Dewar and Nolan’s (2013) study demonstrated that even the most transient of contacts can reflect supportive connection between those involved. Indeed, if ‘person knowledge’ – knowing who people are, what matters to them and understanding how they feel – is considered optional in care settings, then such knowledge will continue to be difficult to articulate and celebrate.

Learning points

  1. Practitioners’ self-recognition is crucial to the delivery of effective care. Without such self-knowledge a practitioner’s own emotional response to patients may act as a barrier to effective care.
  2. Developing relationships, emotional connections, interpersonal skills, and knowing the person with the illness have been identified as key processes that enable compassion to be realised in practice.
  3. Three interpersonal processes have been identified: being present, opening eyes and co-creating.
  4. Sharing emotion in the nurse-patient interaction can be viewed as unprofessional. Nurses can ‘rehabilitate’ the emotions deemed to be inappropriate back into the nurse-patient relationship, to enable changing from a model of keeping one’s distance to one of meaningful engagement.
